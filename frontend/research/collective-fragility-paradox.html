Abstract

The Collective Fragility Paradox

Every financial institution that selected CrowdStrike made a rational decision—choosing the market-leading endpoint security platform. Collectively, those rational decisions created a single point of failure affecting 60% of the Fortune 500 when a faulty update crashed 8.5 million systems in 78 minutes.

This is the collective fragility paradox: individually rational vendor selection creates collectively fragile systems.

Traditional TPRM frameworks organize risk around legal entities—vendors assessed through contracts, questionnaires, and SOC 2 reports. But modern technology incidents don't respect organizational boundaries. They propagate through code libraries no one contracted with, build pipelines no questionnaire examines, and infrastructure platforms that appear as "diversified vendors" while sharing identical dependencies.

"The industry manages parties when it should be managing dependencies."

Between 2021 and 2024, seven incidents—Log4j, SolarWinds, MOVEit, CrowdStrike, Kaseya, 3CX, and xz Utils—caused an estimated $30-200 billion in economic damage. Each exploited dependency-level risks that entity-focused frameworks were never designed to see.

This paper examines the structural mismatch, documents the evidence, and proposes a multi-stakeholder path forward—because no single actor can solve this alone.

Key Findings

1

Estimated Economic Damage

Seven incidents between 2021-2024 caused $30-200 billion in combined economic damage—not from vendor failures, but from dependency-level exploits that frameworks weren't designed to detect.

2

The Structural Mismatch

TPRM frameworks organize risk around legal entities. Modern attacks exploit technical dependencies—code libraries, build pipelines, update mechanisms—that exist outside organizational boundaries.

3

Concentration Through Excellence

When 60% of Fortune 500 companies choose the same security platform for good reasons, individually rational decisions create collectively fragile systems.

4

Regulatory Response Timeline

An 18-36 month gap consistently emerges between major incidents and meaningful regulatory guidance, leaving institutions navigating novel risks without clear expectations.

5

The Competency Gap

Traditional risk professionals lack software engineering context; developers lack risk management training. Neither team has the full picture needed for dependency-level assessment.

6

Multi-Stakeholder Solution Required

No single actor—financial institution, vendor, or regulator—can solve this alone. Progress requires coordinated action across the ecosystem with new shared standards.

About the Author

Trevor Kavanaugh

VP, Third-Party Risk Management

With a background in software development and over a decade in banking, Trevor has built his career across the risk spectrum—from compliance and BSA/AML to internal audit, now leading third-party risk management at an FDIC-chartered regional bank. He oversees a portfolio of 500+ vendor relationships and focuses on bridging the gap between traditional vendor oversight and the technical realities of modern software dependencies.

His work challenges the conventional compliance-focused frameworks prevalent across financial services, arguing that the industry manages "parties" when it should be managing "dependencies."
