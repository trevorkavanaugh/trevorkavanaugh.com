# Trevor Kavanaugh LinkedIn Style Blueprint (ARCHIVED)

## Purpose

This document is a comprehensive drafting methodology for writing LinkedIn posts in Trevor Kavanaugh's exact voice. It was derived from close analysis of 9 posts published between December 2025 and March 2026 covering software supply chain risk, SOC 2 analysis, and AI governance. Use this document as the authoritative reference when drafting new LinkedIn posts.

---

## 1. Structure & Format

### 1.1 The Hook (First 2 Lines)

Trevor's hooks follow one core pattern: **a short declarative statement that sounds reasonable, immediately followed by a complication or reframe that creates tension.**

The hook is never a question. It is never a statistic. It is never "I've been thinking about..." It is a confident assertion that sets up the post's central argument.

**Pattern A — State a fact, then destabilize it:**
- "Your vendor didn't write most of their code. And that's not a secret — it's how modern software works."
- "Your vendor's SOC 2 report covers their product. It says so right on the cover. Except it might not."
- "You approved that software three years ago. But that's not what's running on your systems today."

**Pattern B — Concrete analogy, then pivot:**
- "Dell doesn't make their own processors. They buy them from Intel. They don't make their own graphics cards either — those come from Nvidia."

**Pattern C — Bold contrarian claim, then soften with reasoning:**
- "Everyone's talking about AI governance. Frameworks. Guardrails. Responsible AI. Ethical AI. Most of it is buzzwords dressed up as strategy."

**Pattern D — Anecdote that raises a question:**
- "An auditor asked me a simple question last year that I still think about: 'Why are you reviewing 2024 SOC reports in 2025?'"

**Rules for hooks:**
- Always in second person ("your vendor," "you approved") or declarative third ("Dell doesn't make...")
- Maximum 2 sentences before the tension appears
- The hook must be comprehensible to someone who knows nothing about the topic
- Never open with a question, a hashtag, a statistic, or "I"
- The first line should work as a standalone — it must arrest scrolling on its own

### 1.2 The Transition Block (Lines 3-6)

After the hook, Trevor provides 2-4 short sentences that contextualize the claim. This block does three things: (1) gives just enough background so the reader understands what's being discussed, (2) normalizes the status quo ("This is how modern software works"), and (3) sets up the "but" — the pivot into why this matters.

**Examples:**
- "The application you're relying on for core operations? Recent studies from the Linux Foundation show 70-90% of modern software is assembled from open source components — and that number keeps climbing. Your vendor assembled it more than they built it. This isn't a criticism. It's just reality. And it changes what TPRM should be looking at."
- "This is supply chain. Components sourced from specialized manufacturers, assembled into a final product. You already understand this. Modern software works the same way."

**Rules:**
- Keep this section under 5 sentences
- Include one sentence that explicitly validates the current state ("This is normal. This is efficient.")
- End with a pivot line that signals the post's real argument ("But here's where the analogy breaks down." / "And it changes what TPRM should be looking at.")

### 1.3 Section Structure (Emoji Headers)

Posts are divided into 3-5 sections using colored emoji headers. Each section is a self-contained argument block.

**Standard section count:** 3-4 emoji-headed sections per post (not counting the hook/transition).

**Typical section arc:**

| Position | Function | Example Headers |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| Section 1 | Explain the mechanism / how it works | "The persistent channel" / "How this actually works" / "The accountability gap" |
| Section 2 | Explain why it matters / the risk implication | "Why this matters for TPRM" / "Why this is so hard to catch" / "SaaS makes it invisible" |
| Section 3 | Bridge to action or broader context | "The gap in traditional due diligence" / "What this actually means" / "What to actually look for" |
| Section 4 (optional) | Forward-looking / next steps / series teaser | "This is where TPRM is headed" / "Where this is headed" |

### 1.4 Paragraph Structure

- **Short paragraphs.** Most are 1-3 sentences. Never more than 4.
- **Frequent single-sentence paragraphs** used for emphasis, punchlines, or reframes: "If something breaks, there's no one to call." / "That's a rearview mirror." / "The problem is what it leaves out."
- **Rhythm alternation.** A 2-3 sentence explanatory paragraph is followed by a 1-sentence impact paragraph. This creates a teach-then-punch cadence.
- **No paragraph exceeds ~60 words.** LinkedIn is read on phones. Wall-of-text paragraphs are broken up.

### 1.5 The Close (Final Lines)

Every post ends with exactly three elements in this order:

1. **A summary or forward-looking sentence** (often teasing next week's post):
   - "More on this next week — including what visibility into software supply chains actually looks like."
   - "Next week we'll get into the part of the report most people skip..."
   - "Maybe before we race to govern AI, we should finish the last assignment."

2. **A blank line.**

3. **A closing question** directed at the reader — always framed as either/or or "how much do you...":
   - "How much visibility do you have into what's inside your vendors' software — or is that a question you've never asked?"
   - "Does your due diligence account for the difference between these two supply chains — or does it assume they work the same way?"
   - "When you review a SOC 2, how deep do you go into the system description — or do you skip straight to the control results?"
   - "Where should the industry's energy be — catching up on internet-era security gaps, or racing ahead to AI governance?"

**Closing question rules:**
- Always a single question (never two)
- Usually offers a binary: the right way vs. what most people do
- Tone is curious, not accusatory — "or is that a question you've never asked?" not "why aren't you doing this?"
- The question should make the reader slightly uncomfortable if they're honest with themselves

## 2. Voice & Tone

### 2.1 Register

**Informed practitioner talking to peers.** Not academic. Not casual. Not consultant-selling. The register is a senior professional explaining something to other senior professionals who are smart but may not have thought about this specific angle.

Closest analogy: the way a respected colleague explains something at a conference sidebar — direct, confident, no filler, slightly more candid than they'd be on a panel.

### 2.2 Authority Without Preaching

Trevor establishes authority through four specific techniques:

**Technique 1 — He demonstrates knowledge through specificity, not credentials.**
He never says "In my experience..." or "As a VP of..." He proves he knows the subject by naming specific things: "Section III," "trust services criteria," "Log4j," "CUECs," "bridge letters." The specificity IS the credential.

**Technique 2 — He validates the status quo before critiquing it.**
Before saying something is broken, he acknowledges why it exists:
- "That's not a flaw in the standard. It's how SOC 2 was designed."
- "This isn't a criticism. It's just reality."
- "We accept this because there's no better alternative built into the model."

This prevents the post from feeling preachy or condescending. He's not saying people are dumb for doing things this way. He's saying the system has a gap worth examining.

**Technique 3 — He uses "we" and "us" when describing the profession's blind spots.**
- "Most of us have never looked at it."
- "We accept this because there's no better alternative."
- "We still haven't figured out what's already here."

He includes himself in the critique. He's not above the problem. He's part of the profession facing it.

**Technique 4 — He asks questions he clearly already knows the answer to.**
The closing questions aren't genuine uncertainty. They're rhetorical invitations for the reader to confront something. But the phrasing is gentle enough that it reads as curiosity, not challenge.

### 2.3 Person Usage

- **Second person ("you/your") is dominant.** Used to make the reader the protagonist: "Your vendor," "your risk assessment," "your environment."
- **First person plural ("we") is used for shared profession-level observations.** "We ask vendors about their security practices." "We accept this because..."
- **First person singular ("I") is rare.** Used only for personal anecdotes: "An auditor asked me..." / "I don't say that to be dismissive." Appears in 2 of 9 posts.
- **Third person is used for vendors, auditors, and attackers** — the other actors in the story.

### 2.4 Recurring Sentence Patterns

**The "This isn't X. It's Y." reframe:**
- "This isn't a criticism. It's just reality."
- "This isn't optional anymore. It's what the landscape demands."
- "That's not continuous assurance. That's a rearview mirror."
- "That's not a one-time event. That's the new normal."
- "That's not a control gap anyone planned for."

**The staccato list (sentence fragments as a list):**
- "No contract. No SLA. No legal entity. No warranty."
- "No approval step. No change control window."
- "Updates. Patches. New versions."
- "Frameworks. Guardrails. Responsible AI. Ethical AI."

**The "X, not Y" distinction:**
- "Read it like a detective, not a reviewer."
- "We build capture processes, not maintenance processes."
- "You're reading a clean audit of a partial picture."

**The escalating specificity:**
- "Maybe a team at Google. Maybe one guy in Nebraska."
- "Could have been this morning. Could have been an hour ago."
- "Some scoping decisions are obvious... Some are subtler... And some are almost invisible."

**The disarming concession:**
- "I don't say that to be dismissive. I say it because..."
- "This isn't a reason to stop collecting SOC 2s."

### 2.5 What Trevor NEVER Does

- **Never uses first-person bragging.** No "I helped a client..." / "In my 15 years..." / "I recently spoke at..."
- **Never sells or pitches.** No CTA to hire him, buy something, or book a call. Ever.
- **Never uses motivational/inspirational tone.** No "Let's change the industry!" / "We can do better!" / "The future is bright."
- **Never uses hashtags in the body text.** (May use them at the very end for discoverability, but they're not part of the post's voice.)
- **Never hedges with weak qualifiers.** No "I think maybe" / "It could potentially" / "In some cases, perhaps." He makes declarative statements.
- **Never addresses the reader as a student.** No "Here's what you need to know" / "Let me explain" / "5 things every TPRM professional should..."
- **Never uses listicle format.** No numbered tips. No "Top 10." No "3 ways to improve your..."
- **Never name-drops his own company or title.**
- **Never uses fake urgency.** No "Act now" / "This is critical" / "You need to do this today."

## 3. Rhetorical Techniques

### 3.1 Making Technical Concepts Accessible

**Primary technique: Concrete analogy from a familiar domain, then show where the analogy breaks.**

The Dell/Intel hardware supply chain analogy (Post 2) is the clearest example. He picks something the reader already understands, maps it cleanly to the technical concept, then explicitly identifies where the comparison fails. The failure point IS the insight.

**Secondary technique: Specific named examples over abstract descriptions.**

Instead of "a major security vulnerability in an open source component," he says "Log4j." Instead of "a supply chain attack," he says "SolarWinds (2020)." Instead of "a software update failure," he says "CrowdStrike (2024)." The named example does the explaining — readers either know it or can look it up. He doesn't over-explain well-known incidents.

**Tertiary technique: "You already do X — this is the same thing, but..."**

He meets the reader where they are: "You already understand supply chains. Software works the same way." Then he introduces the twist: "But here's where the analogy breaks down."

### 3.2 Handling Contrarian Takes

Trevor never positions himself as a maverick or contrarian. He positions the OBSERVATION as surprising, not himself. The structure is:

1. State what everyone assumes ("Your vendor's SOC 2 covers their product")
2. Reveal the gap calmly ("Except it might not")
3. Explain the mechanism ("the vendor defines the scope")
4. Validate the design ("That's not a flaw in the standard")
5. Show why it still matters ("But that same flexibility means...")

He never says "everyone is wrong." He says "the system has a structural feature most people haven't examined." The critique targets the system, not the people operating within it.

### 3.3 Building Tension

**Progressive revelation.** He starts with an accessible claim and peels back layers:
- Post 5 (SOC 2 Scoping): "Some scoping decisions are obvious... Some are subtler... And some are almost invisible."
- Post 1 (Supply Chain): He goes from "vendors use open source" (known) to "they might not know what's in their own code" (surprising) to "the next Log4j is already out there" (urgent).

**The "And" pile-up.** He stacks implications without resolving them, building pressure:
- "The vendor added services. They changed subcontractors. They obtained new data access. None of this triggered an update."
- "No contract. No SLA. No legal entity. No warranty. If something breaks, there's no one to call."

**Delayed payoff.** In Post 3, he presents two case studies (SolarWinds, CrowdStrike) separately, then delivers the insight: "Same delivery mechanism. Different failure mode." The reader connects the dots.

### 3.4 Use of Questions

Trevor uses questions in exactly three positions:

1. **Mid-post rhetorical questions** that the reader can't answer (exposing the gap): "Did anyone approve those 187 updates individually? Or did they just... happen?"
2. **Rapid-fire diagnostic questions** that force the reader to audit themselves: "Does your team actually monitor those logs? Do you have the staffing? Did anyone even set up the log exports?"
3. **Closing questions** (see Section 1.5) that offer a binary self-assessment.

He never asks questions in the hook. He never asks questions he intends to answer himself (no "So what's the solution? Here's what I think...").

## 4. Content Patterns

### 4.1 Bridging "What Is" to "Why It Matters"

The bridge is always structural, not emotional. He never says "this should scare you" or "this is a huge risk." Instead, he shows the mechanism that creates the risk and lets the reader draw the conclusion.

**The bridge pattern:**
1. Here's how the thing works (mechanism)
2. Here's what that means for the thing you care about (implication)
3. Here's the gap between what you assumed and what's real (delta)

**Example (Post 7 — Timing Gap):**
1. Mechanism: "A SOC 2 Type II covers a historical period... The audit firm issues the report sometime in Q1 2025."
2. Implication: "By the time you're reading that report, you're evaluating controls that were tested months ago."
3. Delta: "That's not continuous assurance. That's a rearview mirror."

### 4.2 Call to Action

There is no call to action in the traditional LinkedIn sense. No "DM me," no "follow for more," no "share this with your team." The closing question IS the engagement mechanism. It invites comment without asking for it. It prompts self-reflection without being prescriptive.

The only structural CTA is the series teaser: "More on this next week" or "Next week we'll cover..." This creates continuity without selling.

### 4.3 References to Specific Events & Data

**Named incidents are preferred over statistics.** SolarWinds, CrowdStrike, and Log4j appear multiple times across the series. They function as shared vocabulary — shorthand the audience already understands.

**When statistics are used, they're stacked for cumulative effect** (Post 9):
- "In 2020... 18,323. By 2023... 29,000. In 2024... 40,000. In 2025 — 48,448. That's a 164% increase in five years."
- Each number is a separate beat. He doesn't embed them in prose. He lets the escalation speak.

**Sources are named but not linked.** "Recent studies from the Linux Foundation show..." — not a hyperlink, not a footnote. The citation gives credibility without breaking flow.

### 4.4 Frameworks, Regulations, and Vendors

- **SOC 2** is referenced extensively (Posts 5-8) — always with specific structural knowledge (Section III, Section IV, trust services criteria, CUECs, bridge letters, Type II).
- **AICPA** is named once, in context.
- **OCC, FFIEC** are never mentioned (he doesn't cite regulations by name unless directly relevant).
- **Specific vendors** are named only as case studies (SolarWinds, CrowdStrike, Dell, Intel, Nvidia), never as recommendations or endorsements.
- **No proprietary frameworks or methodologies** are promoted. He doesn't name his own approach or brand it.

### 4.5 Series Continuity

Trevor writes in multi-post series with explicit connective tissue:

**Forward references:** "More on this next week — including what visibility into software supply chains actually looks like." / "Over the next few posts, we're going to walk through each of these layers."

**Backward references:** "Last week we talked about how vendors draw system boundaries..." / "We've talked about what's inside software — the components, the open source supply chain."

**Series arc:** Each series follows a logical progression from concept introduction to deeper examination to practical implications. The Software Supply Chain series goes: what's in the code → how it compares to hardware → how it's delivered → the persistent update channel. The SOC 2 series goes: scoping problem → system description → timing gap → CUECs.

**Rule:** When writing a post that's part of a series, include one sentence linking back to the previous post and one sentence teasing the next (unless it's the final post in the series).

## 5. Emoji Usage

### 5.1 Which Emojis

Trevor uses exactly 5 emojis, always as section headers:

| Emoji | Meaning | Frequency |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| 🔴 | Primary concept / mechanism / "how it works" | Every post (9/9) |
| ⚠️ | Risk implication / "why this matters" / complication | Every post (9/9) |
| 📋 | Practical gap / due diligence implication | 6/9 posts |
| 💡 | Insight / forward-looking / "where this is headed" | 3/9 posts |
| 📊 | Data / statistics / quantified impact | 2/9 posts |

### 5.2 Structural, Not Decorative

Emojis are ONLY used as the first character of a section header. They are never used:
- Inline within paragraphs
- As bullet points
- As decorative flourishes
- In the hook or closing
- More than once per section

**Format:** `[emoji] [Section title in sentence case]` — always followed by a blank line, then the section content.

**Examples:**
- `🔴 The persistent channel`
- `⚠️ Why this matters for TPRM`
- `📋 The gap in traditional due diligence`
- `💡 This is where TPRM is headed`
- `📊 What this means in practice`

### 5.3 Ordering Pattern

The emoji order follows a consistent argumentative arc:
1. 🔴 — Establish the mechanism (what's happening)
2. ⚠️ — Surface the risk (why it matters)
3. 📋 or 📊 — Connect to practice (what it means for your work)
4. 💡 — (Optional) Forward look (where this is headed)

Never lead with ⚠️ or 💡. Always lead with 🔴.

## 6. Anti-Patterns (What Would Sound Wrong)

### 6.1 Things That Would Immediately Sound Un-Trevor

| Anti-Pattern | Why It's Wrong | What He'd Do Instead |
|-------------|----------------|---------------------|
| "5 Tips for Better Vendor Risk Management" | Listicle format; patronizing | Examine one structural issue in depth |
| "I'm excited to share..." | LinkedIn performative enthusiasm | Jump straight to the hook |
| "Thoughts? Let me know below!" | Generic engagement bait | Specific binary closing question |
| "As a TPRM leader with 15 years experience..." | Credential-leading | Let specificity of content demonstrate expertise |
| "This is why you NEED to..." | Prescriptive/salesy urgency | "This is where TPRM is headed" — observational |
| "Here's my hot take:" | Self-aware contrarianism is cringe | State the observation matter-of-factly |
| "Agree? Disagree? 👇" | Low-effort engagement farming | Thoughtful either/or question |
| "TPRM professionals, listen up!" | Addressing audience directly like a crowd | Second person "your vendor," "your risk assessment" |
| "I'm proud to announce..." | Self-promotional | He doesn't announce things about himself |
| "Let's unpack this." | Overused LinkedIn phrase | Just... unpack it. Without announcing that you're about to. |
| "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..." | Cliche opener | Specific concrete claim |
| "Food for thought 🤔" | Vapid sign-off | Either a series teaser or nothing |

### 6.2 Tone Traps to Avoid

- **Don't be alarmist.** Trevor identifies gaps calmly. "That's a rearview mirror" is more effective than "THIS IS A HUGE BLIND SPOT." The understatement is the style.
- **Don't moralize.** He never tells people they should feel bad about the gap. He just shows them the gap.
- **Don't simplify for LinkedIn engagement.** He doesn't dumb down SOC 2 concepts. He makes them accessible through structure and analogy while keeping the technical precision.
- **Don't be comprehensive.** Each post covers ONE angle of ONE topic. He doesn't try to solve the problem in a single post.
- **Don't use corporate-speak.** No "leverage," "synergize," "best-in-class," "paradigm shift," "move the needle."
- **Don't hedge.** "Your vendor may not even know everything that's in their own codebase" — not "It's possible that some vendors might not have complete visibility."

## 7. Drafting Checklist

Use this checklist before finalizing any post:

### Structure
- [ ] Hook is 1-2 declarative sentences with immediate tension
- [ ] Transition block validates status quo before pivoting
- [ ] 3-4 emoji-headed sections following 🔴 → ⚠️ → 📋/📊 → 💡 arc
- [ ] Paragraphs are 1-3 sentences; no walls of text
- [ ] Total word count is 300-500 (target 350-450)
- [ ] Closing has series teaser (if applicable) + binary question

### Voice
- [ ] Second person dominant ("your vendor," "your environment")
- [ ] No first-person credentials or bragging
- [ ] At least one "This isn't X. It's Y." reframe
- [ ] At least one concession validating the current state
- [ ] No hedging language, no weak qualifiers
- [ ] Professional but direct — colleague-to-colleague, not teacher-to-student

### Content
- [ ] One structural issue examined in depth (not a survey of many issues)
- [ ] Named examples over abstract descriptions where possible
- [ ] Statistics stacked for cumulative effect (if used)
- [ ] No sales pitch, no CTA beyond the closing question
- [ ] Series connection (backward and/or forward reference) if part of a series

### Formatting
- [ ] Emojis only in section headers (🔴 ⚠️ 📋 📊 💡)
- [ ] No inline emojis in body text
- [ ] No hashtags in body text
- [ ] Single-sentence paragraphs used for punchlines/reframes
- [ ] Em dashes (—) used for asides and interjections (his preferred punctuation)

## 8. The Core Formula

If you need one sentence to remember Trevor's style:

**State a reasonable-sounding assumption, calmly reveal the structural gap underneath it, explain the mechanism that creates the gap, validate why it exists, and close by asking the reader whether they've ever examined it.**

The posts are not arguments. They are guided tours of things hiding in plain sight.
