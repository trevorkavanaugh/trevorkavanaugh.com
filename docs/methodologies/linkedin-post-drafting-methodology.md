## 2. Structure Blueprint

### 2.1 The Hook (First 1-3 Lines)

Trevor's hooks follow consistent patterns. He never buries the lead. The first line must work as a standalone — it must arrest scrolling by itself.

**Pattern A — Gap Reveal (5 of 25 posts, most common):**
State what the reader assumes, then immediately show it's wrong.

- "Your vendor contract says one company. Your actual risk exposure is with a company you've never assessed." (Post 9)
- "Your vendor didn't write most of their code. And that's not a secret — it's how modern software works." (Post 17)
- "You approved that software three years ago. But that's not what's running on your systems today." (Post 20)
- "Your vendor's SOC 2 report covers their product. It says so right on the cover. Except it might not." (Post 22)
- "You approved the vendor. But did you approve what they're actually doing?" (Post 13)

**Pattern B — Bold Declarative Claim (4 of 25 posts):**
A confident assertion that creates tension.

- "Most banks define 'critical vendors' wrong. And both directions hurt you." (Post 10)
- "Compliance with regulatory guidance should be the baseline, not the goal line." (Post 12)
- "You can build perfect software. But if the way it reaches your systems is compromised, none of that matters." (Post 19)
- "TPRM requirements have increased tenfold. Your headcount won't." (Post 14)

**Pattern C — "Everyone/Nobody" Contrarian Reframe (3 of 25 posts):**
Call out what the crowd is doing, then redirect.

- "Everyone obsesses over the technical integration... Those are the easy parts." (Post 2)
- "Everyone's obsessing over TPRM frameworks and risk methodologies, but no one mentions the biggest threat to your program" (Post 6)
- "Everyone's talking about AI governance. Frameworks. Guardrails. Responsible AI. Ethical AI. Most of it is buzzwords dressed up as strategy." (Post 25)

**Pattern D — Series Callback + Extension (4 of 25 posts, always in part 2+ of a series):**
Reference last week, then go deeper.

- "Last week we talked about transparency with examiners. Here's what determines if they believe your program is mature" (Post 7)
- "Last week we discussed the fourth-party risk problem. But fourth-party isn't the end of the chain." (Post 11)
- "Last week we talked about how vendors aren't the unit of risk — products and services are. But even that doesn't go deep enough." (Post 15)
- "Last week we talked about how vendors draw system boundaries to quietly exclude backend components from their SOC 2." (Post 24)

**Pattern E — Personal Anecdote (2 of 25 posts):**
Rare. Used only when the personal experience IS the insight.

- "A vendor rep reached out yesterday. This is a masterclass in how to permanently blacklist your company." (Post 4)
- "An auditor asked me a simple question last year that I still think about: 'Why are you reviewing 2024 SOC reports in 2025?'" (Post 21)

**Pattern F — Analogy Bridge (1 of 25 posts):**
Start in a familiar domain, then pivot.

- "Dell doesn't make their own processors. They buy them from Intel. This is supply chain. Modern software works the same way. But here's where the analogy breaks down." (Post 18)

**Hook Rules:**
- Maximum 2-3 sentences before the tension appears
- The first line must be comprehensible to someone who knows nothing about the topic
- Never open with a question, a hashtag, a statistic, or "I'm excited to share"
- The word "I" appears in the opening line of only 3 of 25 posts (Posts 1, 3, 4) — and two of those are "I keep seeing/having," not self-promotional
- Second person ("your vendor," "you approved") is the dominant hook voice

### 2.2 The Transition Block (Lines 2-6)

After the hook, 2-5 sentences provide context. This block has three jobs:

1. **Give enough background** so the reader understands what's being discussed
2. **Normalize the status quo** — acknowledge why things are the way they are
3. **Set up the pivot** — the "but" that introduces why this matters

**Examples of the normalization-then-pivot:**
- "That's not a flaw in the standard. It's how SOC 2 was designed — flexible enough to apply across industries, sizes, and architectures. **But** that same flexibility means..." (Post 22)
- "This isn't a criticism. It's just reality. **And** it changes what TPRM should be looking at." (Post 17)
- "I don't say that to be dismissive. I say it **because**..." (Post 25)
- "Regulators expect different things at different scales. That sounds obvious, **but** I see so many shops..." (Post 1)

**Rules:**
- Keep under 5 sentences
- Include one sentence that validates the current state
- End with a pivot line that signals the post's real argument

### 2.3 Section Structure — Emoji Headers vs. Prose-Only

**Critical finding: Only 8 of 25 posts use emoji section headers.** All 8 are from Dec 30, 2025 onward (Posts 17, 18, 20, 21, 22, 23, 24, 25). The first 16 posts use no emoji headers at all — they are continuous prose with paragraph breaks and occasional bold sub-headers or labeled sections (e.g., "The reseller shell game:" or "$500M Bank:").

**Emoji header format (for posts using them):**

| Emoji | Function | Appears in |
|-------|----------|------------|
| 🔴 | Primary mechanism / "how this works" | 8/8 emoji posts (always first) |
| ⚠️ | Risk implication / "why this matters" | 6/8 emoji posts (always second) |
| 📋 | Practical gap / "what to look for" / due diligence implication | 6/8 emoji posts (usually third) |
| 📊 | Data / statistics / quantified impact | 3/8 emoji posts (replaces 📋 when data-heavy) |
| 💡 | Forward-looking / "where this is headed" | 3/8 emoji posts (always last when present) |

**Standard emoji section arc:**
1. 🔴 — Establish the mechanism (what's happening)
2. ⚠️ — Surface the risk (why it matters)
3. 📋 or 📊 — Connect to practice (what it means for your work)
4. 💡 — (Optional) Forward look (where this is headed)

**Emoji usage rules:**
- Emojis are ONLY used as the first character of a section header
- Never used inline within paragraphs, as bullet points, or as decoration
- Never used in the hook or closing
- Format: `🔴 Section title in sentence case` — followed by blank line, then content
- 🔴 always appears first. Never lead with ⚠️ or 💡
- 3-4 emoji sections is the standard. Post 21 (SOC 2 Timing Gap) uses all 4 emojis; most use 3

**For prose-only posts (the Nov–early Dec style):**
- Sections are separated by blank lines and paragraph breaks
- Sub-topics are introduced with bold text or labeled headers (e.g., "The root cause?", "What actually works?", "WHAT ACTUALLY WORKS:")
- Tiered lists use labeled entries (e.g., "$500M Bank:", "Level 1:", "Over-concentration:")

**Recommendation for new posts:** Use the emoji header format (the current mature style). Fall back to prose-only for very short posts (<200 words) or rapid-response posts.

### 2.4 Paragraph Structure

- **Short paragraphs.** Most are 1-3 sentences. Never more than 4.
- **Single-sentence paragraphs** are used frequently for emphasis, punchlines, or reframes:
  - "The problem is what it leaves out." (Post 23)
  - "That's not a control gap anyone planned for." (Post 24)
  - "Programs look sophisticated on paper. The data underneath is garbage." (Post 6)
  - "That's not monitoring — that's archaeology." (Post 7)
  - "The invoice doesn't tell you where the risk is. It just tells you who to pay." (Post 9)
- **Rhythm alternation:** A 2-3 sentence explanatory paragraph is followed by a 1-sentence impact paragraph. This teach-then-punch cadence is the heartbeat of the style.
- **No paragraph exceeds ~60 words.** LinkedIn is read on phones.

### 2.5 The Close (Final 1-4 Lines)

Every post ends with a question directed at the reader. The closing patterns fall into distinct categories:

**Type A — "What's been your experience?" (8 of 25 posts — the most common):**
Simple, open-ended. Used for standalone posts and early series entries.

- "What's been your experience with this?" (Posts 1, 3, 8, 10)
- "What's been your experience with reseller relationships?" (Post 9)
- "What's been your experience with audit and examination transparency?" (Post 5)
- "What's your experience?" (Posts 12, 16)

**Type B — Binary Self-Assessment (5 of 25 posts):**
Offers two options — the right way vs. what most people actually do. The reader must admit which one they are.

- "How much visibility do you have into what's inside your vendors' software — or is that a question you've never asked?" (Post 17)
- "When you review a SOC 2, how deep do you go into the system description — or do you skip straight to the control results?" (Post 22)
- "Does your team review CUECs and map them against what you're actually doing — or is that section getting skimmed?" (Post 24)
- "How much time does your team spend on the system description versus the control results — and has it ever changed a risk decision?" (Post 23)
- "Do you rely on bridge letters, or have you found other ways to fill it?" (Post 21)

**Type C — Direct Self-Assessment (4 of 25 posts):**
A pointed question that forces the reader to evaluate their own program.

- "Where does your program actually operate?" (Post 7)
- "How much do you know about how your critical vendors push updates to your environment?" (Post 19)
- "When was the last time you knew — actually knew — what changed in an update before it hit your environment?" (Post 20)
- "When's the last time your due diligence went below the application layer?" (Post 15)

**Type D — "How does your program handle this?" (2 of 25 posts):**
- Posts 13 and 14. Simple and open.

**Type E — Industry/Opinion Question (2 of 25 posts):**
- "Where do you think the industry needs to start?" (Post 11)
- "Where should the industry's energy be — catching up on internet-era security gaps, or racing ahead to AI governance?" (Post 25)

**Type F — Dual-Audience (1 of 25 posts):**
- "Buyers: What gets your attention? Sellers: What's worked without burning bridges?" (Post 4)

**Closing question rules:**
- Always a single question (or binary pair)
- Tone is curious, not accusatory
- The question should make the reader slightly uncomfortable if they're honest
- No "Thoughts? 👇", no "Agree?", no "Share this"
- The only structural CTA is a series teaser before the question: "More on this next week..." or "Next week we'll get into..."

**Evolution note:** Early posts (Nov) favor the generic "What's been your experience?" close. Later posts (Feb–Mar) favor the binary self-assessment format, which is more engaging and specific. **New posts should use the binary self-assessment format.**

## 3. Voice DNA

### 3.1 Register

**Informed practitioner talking to peers.** Not academic. Not casual. Not consultant-selling. The register is a senior professional explaining something to other senior professionals who are smart but may not have thought about this specific angle.

Closest analogy: the way a respected colleague explains something at a conference sidebar — direct, confident, no filler, slightly more candid than they'd be on a panel.

### 3.2 Person Usage

| Person | Usage | Frequency | Examples |
|--------|-------|-----------|---------|
| **2nd person** ("you/your") | Dominant voice. Makes the reader the protagonist. | 23 of 25 posts | "Your vendor," "your risk assessment," "your environment" |
| **1st person plural** ("we") | Shared profession-level observations. Includes himself in the critique. | ~10 posts | "We accept this because there's no better alternative." "We've outsourced our risk management to regulators." "We still haven't figured out what's already here." |
| **1st person singular** ("I") | Rare. Only for personal anecdotes or concessions. | ~6 posts | "I keep having the same conversation," "I spent two years in internal audit," "I don't say that to be dismissive" |
| **3rd person** | For vendors, auditors, regulators, and banks as case studies. | Throughout | "Bank A's BSA team flags every transaction," "The vendor defines the scope" |

**Key rule:** He includes himself in professional critiques using "we" but never centers himself as the hero. "I" is used to admit something, share an observation, or make a concession — never to brag.

## 4. Content Formula

### 4.1 How He Introduces a Problem

He never introduces a problem by telling you it's a problem. He describes a situation that sounds normal, then reveals the structural gap hiding inside it. The reader discovers the problem alongside him rather than being told about it.

**The pattern:**
1. Here's what everyone does / assumes (normalization)
2. Here's the structural reason that assumption is incomplete (mechanism)
3. Here's what that means for your program (implication)

**Example — Post 6 (Data Rot):**
1. "You've got elegant frameworks and comprehensive policies that satisfy regulator expectations." (normalization)
2. "But the data that drives those frameworks is slowly rotting away." (mechanism reveal)
3. "Banks build elegant risk assessments on top of stale data, and nobody catches it until an auditor asks..." (implication)

### 4.2 How He Explains the Mechanism

Trevor teaches through specificity. He doesn't explain concepts abstractly — he walks through exactly how something works, step by step, using concrete scenarios.

**Techniques:**
- **Named scenario:** "Critical vendor with full NPPI access performing internal audit functions. Business unit wrote 'consulting services' on the intake form." (Post 6)
- **Temporal walkthrough:** "A SOC 2 Type II covers a historical period — say January through December 2024. The audit firm issues the report sometime in Q1 2025..." (Post 21)
- **Progressive peeling:** Start with the obvious layer, then go deeper. "Some scoping decisions are obvious... Some are subtler... And some are almost invisible." (Post 22)

### 4.3 How He Bridges to "Why It Matters"

The bridge is always structural, not emotional. He never says "this should scare you" or "this is a huge risk." He shows the mechanism that creates the risk and lets the reader draw the conclusion.

**Bridge pattern:**
1. Here's how the thing works (mechanism)
2. Here's what that means for the thing you care about (implication)
3. Here's the gap between what you assumed and what's real (delta)

**Example — Post 21 (Timing Gap):**
1. Mechanism: "A SOC 2 Type II covers a historical period... The audit firm issues the report sometime in Q1 2025."
2. Implication: "By the time you're reading that report, you're evaluating controls that were tested months ago."
3. Delta: "That's not continuous assurance. That's a rearview mirror."

### 4.4 How He Handles Data and Statistics

- **Named incidents preferred over raw numbers.** SolarWinds, CrowdStrike, Log4j function as shared vocabulary.
- **When statistics are used, they're stacked for escalation** — each number is a separate beat, building pressure:
  - "In 2020... 18,323. By 2023... 29,000. In 2024... 40,000. In 2025 — 48,448. That's a 164% increase in five years." (Post 25)
  - "$225 million breakup fee. $3.09 billion in penalties. An asset cap that killed future growth." (Post 3)
- **Statistics are embedded in specific stories,** not presented as abstract figures. "$13.4 billion First Horizon acquisition" — the number serves the narrative.
- **Sources are named but not linked.** "Recent studies from the Linux Foundation show..." (Post 17). The citation gives credibility without breaking flow.

### 4.5 How He References Industry Events

- Always by name: "SolarWinds (2020)," "CrowdStrike (2024)," "Log4j" — never "a recent major breach"
- The reference assumes the reader knows the basic story. He doesn't over-explain well-known incidents.
- Events are used to illustrate a structural point, not for shock value. The SolarWinds/CrowdStrike comparison in Post 19 exists to show "Same delivery mechanism. Different failure mode."
- Regulatory events are named precisely: "DORA went live in the EU almost a year ago" (Post 16), "Dodd-Frank's enhanced prudential standards" (Post 1)

### 4.6 How He Handles Series Continuity

Trevor writes in multi-post series with explicit connective tissue.

**Series in the corpus:**
| Series | Posts | Arc |
|--------|-------|-----|
| Size & Complexity | 1, 3 | Right-sizing → When to build ahead |
| Transparency with Auditors | 5, 7 | Why be transparent → How examiners measure maturity |
| 4th Party Risk | 9, 11 | Reseller gap → Dependency chain convergence |
| Development of 3P Risk | 13, 15 | Vendor ≠ risk unit → Product-level residual risk |
| Software Supply Chain | 17, 18, 19, 20 | What's inside → Composition → Delivery → Persistent channel |
| SOC 2 Analysis | 22, 23, 24 | Scoping → System description → CUECs |

**Forward references:** "More on this next week..." / "Over the next few posts, we're going to walk through each of these layers." / "Next week we'll get into the part of the report most people skip."

**Backward references:** "Last week we talked about..." / "A few weeks ago I posted about..." / "We've talked about what's inside software..."

**Rule:** When writing a post that's part of a series:
- Part 2+: Open with a one-sentence callback to the previous post
- Non-final parts: Include a forward tease before the closing question
- Final part: No forward tease needed — close with a summative statement and the question

### 4.7 His Relationship with the Audience

Trevor treats the audience as **peers who are smart but haven't examined this specific angle.** He is simultaneously:

- **A peer** — "We accept this because there's no better alternative." He includes himself in the profession's blind spots.
- **A guide** — He structures the insight so the reader discovers it along the way. But he never positions himself as a teacher. No "Let me explain" or "Here's what you need to know."
- **An insider** — He demonstrates knowledge through specificity (naming Section III, CUECs, bridge letters), not through credentials. The specificity IS the credential.

He **never** positions himself as:
- A salesperson (no CTAs, no pitches, no "DM me")
- A guru ("In my 15 years...")
- A scold ("You need to do better")
- Above the problem ("I figured this out, let me teach you")

## 6. Anti-Patterns (DO NOT DO)

### 6.1 Things That Would Immediately Sound Un-Trevor

| Anti-Pattern | Why It's Wrong | What He'd Do Instead |
|-------------|----------------|---------------------|
| "5 Tips for Better Vendor Risk Management" | Listicle format; patronizing | Examine one structural issue in depth |
| "I'm excited to share..." | LinkedIn performative enthusiasm | Jump straight to the hook |
| "Thoughts? Let me know below!" | Generic engagement bait | Specific binary closing question |
| "As a TPRM leader with 15 years experience..." | Credential-leading | Let specificity of content demonstrate expertise |
| "This is why you NEED to..." | Prescriptive/salesy urgency | "This is where TPRM is headed" — observational |
| "Here's my hot take:" | Self-aware contrarianism is cringe | State the observation matter-of-factly |
| "Agree? Disagree? 👇" | Low-effort engagement farming | Thoughtful either/or question |
| "TPRM professionals, listen up!" | Addressing audience directly like a crowd | Second person "your vendor," "your risk assessment" |
| "I'm proud to announce..." | Self-promotional | He doesn't announce things about himself |
| "Let's unpack this." | Overused LinkedIn phrase | Just unpack it without announcing it |
| "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..." | Cliche opener | Specific concrete claim |
| "Food for thought 🤔" | Vapid sign-off | Binary self-assessment question |
| "🚨 BREAKING:" or "⚡ URGENT:" | Alarm emoji usage | Calm, specific observation |
| "Here are 3 things I learned..." | Numbered takeaway format | One deep structural insight |
| "What do you think? Comment below!" | Explicit engagement solicitation | Implicit engagement via thoughtful question |

### 6.2 Tone Traps to Avoid

- **Don't be alarmist.** Trevor identifies gaps calmly. "That's a rearview mirror" is more effective than "THIS IS A HUGE BLIND SPOT." Understatement is the style.
- **Don't moralize.** He never tells people they should feel bad. He shows them the gap.
- **Don't simplify for engagement.** He doesn't dumb down SOC 2 concepts. He makes them accessible through structure and analogy while keeping technical precision.
- **Don't be comprehensive.** Each post covers ONE angle of ONE topic. He doesn't solve the problem in a single post.
- **Don't use corporate-speak.** No "leverage," "synergize," "best-in-class."
- **Don't hedge.** "Your vendor may not even know everything that's in their own codebase" — not "It's possible that some vendors might not have complete visibility."
- **Don't use exclamation points in arguments.** He never raises his voice. Even strong conclusions use periods.
- **Don't summarize the post at the end.** No "In conclusion" or "To wrap up" or "Key takeaways." The closing question IS the ending.
- **Don't use emoji inline.** If you're using emoji, they're section headers only. Never scattered through paragraphs.

### 6.3 Common AI Writing Tells to Avoid

| AI Tell | Trevor's Version |
|---------|-----------------|
| "It's important to note that..." | Just state it |
| "This underscores the need for..." | Show the mechanism; the need is self-evident |
| "In the ever-changing world of..." | Name the specific change |
| "Organizations must ensure..." | "Your program" — second person, specific |
| "It goes without saying..." | Then don't say it |
| "A robust framework for..." | Name the specific capability |
| "Stakeholders should consider..." | Who specifically? What specifically? |
| Perfect parallel structure in every list | Trevor's lists vary — some are fragments, some sentences, some labeled entries |
| Every paragraph the same length | Trevor alternates 3-sentence explanatory with 1-sentence punchline |
| Excessive hedging: "may," "could," "potentially" | Trevor makes declarative statements |
| Transition words: "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally" | Trevor uses paragraph breaks and em dashes, not formal transitions |
| **"This isn't X. It's Y." / "That's not X — it's Y."** | **RETIRED. This was a genuine Trevor pattern but is now widely flagged as an LLM tell. Rephrase the same insight through concrete examples or direct statements instead of the formulaic reframe.** |

## 7. Drafting Checklist

### Step-by-Step Process for Drafting a New Post

**Step 1: Define the single structural insight.**
- What is the one thing hiding in plain sight?
- Can you state it in one sentence? If not, narrow the topic.
- Is this a standalone post or part of a series?

**Step 2: Write the hook (1-3 lines).**
- Choose a hook pattern (Gap Reveal, Bold Claim, Contrarian Reframe, Series Callback, or Anecdote)
- First line must work as a standalone — would it stop someone scrolling?
- Does the tension appear by line 2-3?

**Step 3: Write the transition block (2-5 sentences).**
- Provide just enough context for a non-expert to follow
- Include one sentence that validates the current state ("This is how it was designed," "That's not lazy — that's proportionate")
- End with the pivot ("But..." / "And it changes..." / "Here's where...")

**Step 4: Write 3-4 emoji-headed sections.**
- 🔴 — How the mechanism works (explain what's happening)
- ⚠️ — Why it matters (surface the risk implication)
- 📋 or 📊 — Connect to practice (what to look for, what it means for due diligence)
- 💡 — (Optional) Where this is headed / forward-looking

**Step 5: Write the closing.**
- If part of a series: Add a one-sentence forward tease before the question
- Write a binary self-assessment question that makes the reader slightly uncomfortable
- Format: "Do you [good practice] — or [what most people actually do]?"

**Step 6: Review for word count.**
- Target: 380-500 words
- If under 350: add one more concrete example or diagnostic question
- If over 500: cut the least essential section

### Quality Gates Before Publishing

**Structure check:**
- [ ] Hook is 1-3 sentences with immediate tension
- [ ] Transition validates status quo, then pivots
- [ ] 3-4 emoji sections following 🔴 → ⚠️ → 📋/📊 → 💡 arc
- [ ] Paragraphs are 1-3 sentences max; no walls of text
- [ ] At least 2 single-sentence paragraphs used for punchlines
- [ ] Word count is 380-500 (or justified if shorter)
- [ ] Closing has a binary self-assessment question

**Voice check:**
- [ ] Second person dominant ("your vendor," "your environment")
- [ ] No first-person bragging or credential-leading
- [ ] No "This isn't X. It's Y." reframes (LLM tell — retired)
- [ ] At least one concession validating the current state
- [ ] No hedging language ("maybe," "potentially," "it could be")
- [ ] No exclamation points in arguments
- [ ] Em dashes used for asides (not parentheses or commas)
- [ ] Professional but direct — colleague-to-colleague, not teacher-to-student

**Content check:**
- [ ] ONE structural issue examined in depth (not a survey of many)
- [ ] Named examples where possible (specific banks, incidents, frameworks)
- [ ] No sales pitch, no CTA beyond the closing question
- [ ] If part of series: backward reference (Part 2+) and/or forward tease (non-final)
- [ ] No "In conclusion" or summary paragraph at the end

**Anti-pattern check:**
- [ ] No listicle format
- [ ] No LinkedIn cliches ("Let's unpack this," "In today's landscape")
- [ ] No AI writing tells ("It's important to note," "Furthermore")
- [ ] No inline emoji — emoji only in section headers
- [ ] No hashtags in body text
- [ ] No generic sign-offs ("Thoughts?", "What do you think?")
- [ ] Does this sound like something Trevor would actually post? Read it out loud.

## 8. The Core Formula

If you need one sentence to remember Trevor's style:

**State a reasonable-sounding assumption, calmly reveal the structural gap underneath it, explain the mechanism that creates the gap, validate why it exists, and close by asking the reader whether they've ever examined it.**

The posts are not arguments. They are guided tours of things hiding in plain sight.
