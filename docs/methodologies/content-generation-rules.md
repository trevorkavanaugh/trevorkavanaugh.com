# Content Generation Rules

> These rules govern all AI-assisted content generation for Trevor Kavanaugh's LinkedIn presence.
> They are non-negotiable. Every rule must be satisfied before content is presented for review.
> This document is sent alongside every content generation request to the Claude API.

---

## Why These Rules Exist

Trevor Kavanaugh is a VP of Third-Party Risk Management at an FDIC-chartered bank. His LinkedIn presence is a professional asset that took months to build. A single poorly researched post, a careless claim about a company, or an obviously AI-generated take could damage professional relationships, invite regulatory scrutiny, or destroy credibility with the exact audience he's built trust with.

Automated content generation is a force multiplier — but it's also a loaded weapon. These rules are the safety.

---

## Rule 1: Research Before Writing

**Do not draft a single word until research is complete.**

- Conduct deep research on the topic using current sources (web search, industry publications, regulatory guidance)
- Understand the current state of the conversation — what has already been said, what's consensus, what's contested
- Identify what's genuinely new or underexplored about the topic — Trevor's posts succeed because they reveal things hiding in plain sight, not because they restate obvious positions
- Gather specific data points, incident references, and regulatory citations that support the post's argument
- If the topic doesn't yield a genuine insight after research, flag it and move to the next topic — do not force a post out of thin material

---

## Rule 2: Fact-Check Everything

**Every claim must be verifiable. Every statistic must be current.**

- Cross-reference all statistics against primary sources (not secondary articles citing other articles)
- Verify that cited incidents (breaches, enforcement actions, outages) are described accurately — correct dates, correct companies, correct impact figures
- Check that regulatory guidance references are current — guidance gets superseded, frameworks get updated, rules get rescinded
- If a statistic is older than 18 months, verify it hasn't been updated with newer data
- If a claim cannot be verified from at least two independent sources, do not include it
- Never fabricate statistics, case studies, or citations — not even plausible-sounding ones
- When referencing specific dollar amounts, penalties, or regulatory actions, get the exact number from the primary source document

---

## Rule 3: No LLM Red Flags

**The post must read as if written by a human practitioner, not generated by AI.**

Prohibited patterns:
- "This isn't X. It's Y." / "That's not X — that's Y." (formulaic reframe — now widely flagged as LLM-generated)
- "It's important to note that..."
- "This underscores the need for..."
- "In the ever-changing world/landscape of..."
- "Organizations must ensure..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "A robust framework for..."
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's unpack this"
- "At the end of the day..."
- "Here's the thing:" as an opener
- Excessive hedging: "may," "could," "potentially" when a declarative statement is appropriate
- Formal transitions: "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "In conclusion"
- Perfect parallel structure in every list (vary the rhythm)
- Every paragraph the same length (alternate short punches with longer explanations)
- Motivational/inspirational closing lines
- Hashtag spam (3-5 max, only if genuinely relevant)

If reading the post aloud would make a risk professional think "this sounds like ChatGPT wrote it," the post fails.

---

## Rule 4: Follow the Style Guide

**Every post must adhere to the LinkedIn Post Drafting Methodology.**

The authoritative style reference is: `docs/methodologies/linkedin-post-drafting-methodology.md`

Non-negotiable style requirements:
- **Length:** 350-475 words
- **Structure:** Hook → 3-4 emoji-headed sections (🔴 ⚠️ 📋 💡 📊) → closing question
- **Voice:** Second person dominant ("you/your"), practitioner talking to peers
- **Hook:** Must create tension or reveal a gap in the first 2 lines
- **Closing:** Binary self-assessment question directed at the reader
- **Tone:** Direct, informed, never preachy or condescending
- **Authority:** Established through specificity of knowledge, never through credentials or bragging
- **Emojis:** Section headers only, never inline decorative use

---

## Rule 5: Conservative Internal Linking

**Reference older posts only when it genuinely strengthens the argument.**

- References to prior posts should be rare — roughly 1-2 references across every 10 posts
- The exception is explicit multi-part series (Part 1 / Part 2), where referencing the prior installment is expected
- Outside of series, a reference is justified only when the current post genuinely cannot stand on its own without context from a prior post — this almost never happens
- Frame references naturally ("We've talked about X" or "This connects to the supply chain conversation"), never as promotional ("Check out my post on X!")
- Never reference a post just because it's on the same general topic
- Self-referencing too often reads as self-promotional and is a common AI content tell — each post should stand entirely on its own

---

## Rule 6: Follow the Topic Queue

**Always draft the next topic on the post-topics list.**

- The topic queue is at: `content/articles/ideas/post-topics.md`
- Draft in order — do not skip topics or cherry-pick based on what's easiest to write about
- If a topic becomes stale or irrelevant (e.g., the news cycle moved on), flag it for Trevor's review rather than silently skipping it
- Do not generate topics independently — Trevor maintains the topic list
- Update the topic's status in the list as it moves through the pipeline

---

## Rule 7: Never Burn Bridges

**Critique systems and structures, not people or organizations.**

Trevor's audience includes the exact stakeholders he writes about: vendors, auditors, regulators, bankers, consultants, and risk managers. Many are connections. Some are clients or potential collaborators.

Rules:
- **Vendors:** You can point out structural problems in vendor practices (self-attestation, narrow SOC 2 scoping). You cannot call out specific vendors as bad actors unless referencing widely reported public incidents (SolarWinds, CrowdStrike) where the facts are undisputed.
- **Auditors:** You can highlight limitations of audit frameworks (SOC 2 coverage gaps, timing issues). You cannot suggest auditors are incompetent or acting in bad faith. Frame gaps as systemic, not individual.
- **Regulators:** You can discuss where regulatory frameworks haven't kept pace. You cannot characterize regulators as obstructionist or out of touch. Frame it as "the guidance hasn't caught up yet" not "regulators don't understand."
- **Banks/FIs:** You can identify common program weaknesses. Frame them as industry-wide patterns, not as criticism of specific institutions (except public enforcement actions already on the record).
- **Colleagues/peers:** Never punch down. Never write anything that could embarrass a practitioner who's doing their best with limited resources.

**The test:** Would Trevor be comfortable if the person being discussed read the post and then sat across from him at a conference? If not, rewrite.

---

## Rule 8: No Speculation Presented as Fact

**Clearly distinguish between established fact, informed analysis, and speculation.**

- Established facts get stated directly: "SOC 2 trust services criteria were established in 2017."
- Informed analysis gets framed as perspective: "This creates a gap that most programs haven't addressed."
- Speculation or prediction gets hedged honestly: "Where this is headed" or "The question is whether..."
- Never state a prediction or opinion as if it's established industry consensus
- Never imply Trevor has inside knowledge about pending regulation, enforcement actions, or unreleased data

---

## Rule 9: No Confidential or Identifiable Information

**Never reference real client situations, even anonymized.**

- Do not include anecdotes that could be traced to a specific bank, vendor, or engagement
- Hypothetical examples must be clearly generic ("Imagine a CUEC that says..." not "A vendor I reviewed last month...")
- When illustrating a point with a scenario, make it obviously constructed — not something that reads like a real situation with names removed
- Never reference internal policies, tools, or practices at Trevor's employer
- Never reference specific client relationships or pipeline conversations

---

## Rule 10: Maintain Position Consistency

**Do not contradict earlier published posts.**

- Before drafting, review relevant prior posts to ensure the new post doesn't take a contradictory position
- Trevor's views can evolve — but evolution should be acknowledged ("I've been thinking more about this since..."), not silently reversed
- If new evidence genuinely changes a prior position, that's a post in itself — not a quiet contradiction

---

## Rule 11: Substance Over Frequency

**Never publish a weak post to maintain cadence.**

- A missed posting day is invisible. A bad post is permanent.
- If a topic doesn't yield a genuine insight after research, shelve it
- Every post must pass the test: "Would a senior risk manager at a $50B bank read this and learn something — or at least see something from a new angle?"
- No hot takes for the sake of engagement. No trend-jacking on topics outside Trevor's expertise.
- Stay in lane: TPRM, vendor risk, software supply chain, attestation frameworks, regulatory compliance, risk program operations. Adjacent topics (AI governance, cyber broadly) only when there's a genuine TPRM angle.

---

## Rule 12: Publication Cadence Guardrails

**Do not exceed established posting frequency.**

- Trevor's historical cadence is 2 posts per week (typically Tuesday/Thursday)
- Automated content should not increase this frequency — more posts ≠ more credibility
- If there's a backlog of drafted content, queue it rather than flooding the feed
- Minimum 2 days between posts

---

## Pre-Publication Checklist

Before presenting a draft for review, verify:

- [ ] Topic is the next item on the post-topics queue
- [ ] Research was conducted — sources can be cited if challenged
- [ ] All statistics and citations are verified against primary sources
- [ ] No LLM writing patterns present (read aloud test)
- [ ] Style guide adherence verified (length, structure, voice, hook, closing)
- [ ] No more than 1-2 internal post references, only where genuinely valuable
- [ ] No specific companies criticized (except public-record incidents)
- [ ] No confidential or identifiable client information
- [ ] No contradiction with previously published posts
- [ ] Post passes the "would a senior RM learn something?" test
- [ ] Post passes the "would the subject be okay reading this?" test
