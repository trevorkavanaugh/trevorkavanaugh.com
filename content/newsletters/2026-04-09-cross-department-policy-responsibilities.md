# Cross-Department Policy Responsibilities: The Accountability Gap

The most dangerous line in any TPRM policy is a responsibility assigned to a department that isn't yours. It looks like coverage on paper. It satisfies a reviewer scanning the document for completeness. But when a regulator asks why that responsibility wasn't executed, they're reading *your* policy - which means they're looking at you, regardless of which department was supposed to carry it out. This is the structural flaw that sinks otherwise solid programs: **policy obligations that cross department lines without corresponding accountability on the other side**.


## What This Looks Like in Practice


At a previous institution, our TPRM policy stated that critical-risk vendors - 13 of them - would receive annual site visits. These were SaaS providers handling sensitive data and core banking functions. Reasonable requirement. Appropriate for the risk tier.


My team of two wasn't qualified to conduct site visits at software companies and assess technical controls. That's not a TPRM program manager's skillset - that's cybersecurity. So I operated under the reasonable assumption that IT was handling it. Our risk management functions ran in silos with minimal cross-department communication, and I had no visibility into whether those visits were actually happening.


They weren't.


The last site visit IT conducted was two years prior, during onboarding of a new MSSP. Even our core processor may have never been visited. Neither had the other 12 critical vendors on the list - all software companies, all handling our most sensitive data.


Our policy said annually. Reality was never.


## Why This Keeps Happening


When I raised it with IT, they didn't dispute the finding. They also didn't change anything. No urgency. No remediation plan. And that reaction made complete sense from their perspective - site visits weren't in *their* policy. They weren't in their objectives. They weren't tied to IT's performance metrics or audit findings. It was a line item in my department's policy that assigned them a responsibility they never agreed to own.


That's the structural problem. Policy statements that assign obligations across department lines without corresponding accountability mechanisms on the receiving end have no enforcement path. When a department doesn't execute a responsibility your policy assigns them, what happens? Usually nothing. There's no violation tracking. No escalation mechanism. No consequence for noncompliance by the department that was supposed to execute.


Your policy says IT conducts site visits. IT's policy says nothing about it. When the examiner asks why site visits weren't completed, they're reading your policy - so they're looking at you.


## Where Else This Pattern Appears


Site visits are one example, but this pattern shows up across every TPRM program that operates in a siloed environment. Consider how many of your policy commitments depend on departments that may not know, may not care, or may not have capacity to comply:



- Your policy says business units will notify TPRM before engaging new vendors. Business units have a dozen competing priorities and no process for remembering to tell you.

- Your policy says IT will provide access certifications for critical vendor connections. IT's ticket queue doesn't have a line item for TPRM access reviews.

- Your policy says procurement will route contracts through your review process. Procurement's goal is closing deals, not slowing them down for risk review.

- Your policy says Legal will flag indemnification provisions that create third-party risk exposure. Legal is managing a contract backlog and doesn't think in TPRM terms.



Each of these is a dependency your program owns on paper but doesn't control operationally. The gap between what your policy promises and what actually happens is where [compliance theater](compliance-theater-vs-real-risk-management.html) lives - documents that look right but don't reflect reality.


## The Fix Nobody Wants to Implement


The second line of defense has a tool for exactly this situation: **violation tracking**. When a department fails to execute a responsibility your policy assigns them, document it. Issue it formally through your risk management framework. Create a paper trail that separates your program's accountability from their noncompliance.


Most TPRM practitioners don't do this because it feels adversarial. It creates friction with colleagues you need to work with every day. It's politically uncomfortable to formally document that IT missed a control requirement for two consecutive years. So programs absorb the gap informally, and the policy continues to promise something that isn't happening.


But the alternative is absorbing the finding when their failure surfaces in your examination. [Regulators who find gaps you didn't disclose](transparency-auditors-regulators-pt1.html) view that as a credibility problem. Your ability to distinguish between a gap your program owns versus a gap caused by another department's non-execution is a different and more defensible position than simply having no explanation for why a policy requirement went unmet.


## Building Enforcement Into the Structure


Violation tracking is the corrective mechanism. But the better solution is building accountability into the policy structure itself before gaps accumulate.


If your policy assigns a responsibility to another department, that department should have a corresponding obligation in their own policy, their own objectives, or a formal MOU that creates accountability on their side. If you can't get that alignment, the honest move is to revise your policy to reflect what actually happens - not what you wish would happen. A policy that accurately describes your program's real controls is more defensible than a policy that describes aspirational controls nobody owns.


Before your next policy review cycle, audit every cross-department dependency in your program. For each one, ask: does the responsible department know they own this? Is it in their policy or their performance metrics? Is there any mechanism that would surface non-execution before an examiner finds it?


If the answer is no to all three, you don't have a control. You have a policy statement.
