# Discovering Shadow Vendors

When I took over vendor management at a previous institution, one question kept nagging at me: what if a business unit simply signed a contract with a new vendor and routed the invoices to Accounts Payable? How would the TPRM function ever know? That question turned into a formal investigation. The answer was worse than I expected - over 150 entities were operating as active vendors with no due diligence on file. No risk assessments. No certificates of insurance. Nothing from my department. Because nobody had ever told us they existed.


## Finding the Gap


I asked around when I first started. Nobody had a clear answer about what happened when a business unit engaged a vendor outside the formal intake process. Some people vaguely thought there was an integration or an approval workflow somewhere. There wasn't.


Accounts Payable had no obligation to check with vendor management before paying a new entity for the first time. No form. No approval workflow. No trigger that would route a new payee through our risk review process. If a business unit engaged a vendor and never told us, that vendor could operate indefinitely - receiving payments, accessing systems, handling data - with zero due diligence, no insurance verification, and no risk assessment. And we'd have no idea.


I reported it as a management self-identified issue. The head of ERM told me it would be a waste of time and effort that wouldn't lead to any discoveries. My intuition said otherwise. If a gap exists, things fall through it.


I worked with AP to build a new vendor approval form. Any new entity that didn't meet specific exemption criteria - SBA referrals, employee reimbursements, and similar one-time disbursements - had to be approved by my department before AP would process the invoice. That closed the gap going forward.


But I still needed to know what had already fallen through.


## Looking Backward: The AP Data Analysis


Vendors bill monthly, quarterly, or annually. It's rare for a vendor to bill on a cycle longer than 12 months. That means 12 months of AP payment data would surface virtually every active vendor the institution was using at that point in time.


My first approach was to ask AP to run every entity through the new approval form retroactively for the prior 12 months. They declined - too much manual work, not enough capacity. So I built an alternative process: compare 12 months of AP payment data against our vendor inventory. Every entity AP had paid that didn't appear in our system got flagged and manually classified into one of four buckets:



- **Known vendor** - in our inventory under a different name or legal entity

- **Possible vendor** - payment pattern suggests an ongoing service relationship

- **Former vendor** - previously in inventory, relationship ended but payments continued

- **Not a vendor** - one-time disbursement, exempt category, or clearly non-vendor



The results came back. Over 150 entities appeared to be active vendors with no due diligence on file.


## What Happened Next


I reported to the risk committee that 150+ entities believed to be vendors were operating outside our policies and procedures. The head of ERM who had told me this would be a waste of time was sitting in that meeting.


The finding was elevated to senior leadership with a directive that it needed to be resolved immediately. I had been trying to get business units to complete reconciliation forms for months with no traction - the typical friction of asking people to do administrative work that isn't their priority. The escalation gave me a direct path to the COO and CEO. With that pressure behind it, the entire list was remediated within 90 days.


The gap ERM said wasn't worth investigating had 150 things fall through it.


What came next was its own lesson. After remediation, [the auditor who reviewed the finding demanded a "preventative" control](preventative-vs-detective-controls.html) - an approval gate that would stop unapproved vendors from entering the payment stream in the first place, rather than relying on the detective analysis I had built. That distinction matters, and it's worth understanding before you face the same question.


## Why This Gap Is More Common Than It Should Be


The shadow vendor problem isn't a failure of intent. Nobody in those business units decided to circumvent risk management. They engaged vendors the same way they always had - found a service they needed, negotiated terms, and sent an invoice to AP. The TPRM function was simply never built into that workflow.


This is a structural problem, not a behavior problem. The fix isn't training business units to "remember" to submit intake forms. The fix is making vendor intake a required step in the payment flow - so it's impossible to pay a new entity without triggering a review. That's [the difference between a control that actually manages risk and one that only creates documentation](compliance-theater-vs-real-risk-management.html).


The backward-looking analysis matters for the same reason. Even after you close the gap prospectively, you don't know what's already operating outside your program. A one-time AP reconciliation - comparing payment history against your inventory - gives you a ground-truth picture of your actual vendor population versus the one you think you have. For most institutions that haven't done this, the number will surprise you.


## Questions Worth Asking Your Program


The self-assessment questions this experience raised aren't complicated, but they're worth working through honestly:



- Does AP have any obligation to notify vendor management before paying a new entity?

- Could a business unit engage a vendor today without triggering your intake process?

- When did you last compare your AP payment history against your vendor inventory?

- If a vendor has been operating outside your program for 18 months, how would you know?



Most TPRM programs are built to manage the vendors they know about. The harder question - and the more important one - is whether the vendors in your system represent your actual vendor population, or just the vendors who went through the front door. [Vendor data degrades over time even for known vendors](information-system-data-rot.html). For shadow vendors, the degradation starts at zero.
