# Finish the Last Assignment - LinkedIn Original

**Posted:** March 10, 2026
**Platform:** LinkedIn
**Author:** Trevor Kavanaugh

---

Everyone's talking about AI governance. Frameworks. Guardrails. Responsible AI. Ethical AI.

Most of it is buzzwords dressed up as strategy.

I don't say that to be dismissive. I say it because for anyone to tell you what AI governance actually looks like in practice, they'd need a crystal ball. We don't know what these systems will be doing in three years, what they'll be integrated into, or what failure modes look like at scale. Building governance frameworks for AI right now is like planning your teenager's curfew when they're still in preschool. You don't even know what they'll be into yet.

And while everyone races to govern what's coming next, we still haven't figured out what's already here.

🔴 The internet era is not secured

In 2020, the security community identified 18,323 new vulnerabilities. By 2023 that hit 29,000. In 2024 it crossed 40,000. In 2025 — 48,448. That's a 164% increase in five years.

Third-party involvement in breaches doubled to 30% this year. Ransomware appeared in 44% of breaches — up 37% year over year. The average data breach now costs $4.88 million.

All from systems we built over the last two decades that we're still struggling to secure.

⚠️ Our attestation tools haven't kept pace

SOC 2's trust services criteria — the foundation of how we independently evaluate vendor controls — were established in 2017 and haven't changed since. SolarWinds happened in 2020. Log4j in 2021. The CrowdStrike outage in 2024.

AICPA designed SOC 2 to assess control environments against defined criteria, and it does that well. But no comparable framework has emerged to cover what SOC 2 was never built for — software supply chain integrity, dependency risk, update delivery mechanisms. The gap from 2017 has only widened.

Now layer AI on top of that.

📊 The speed problem

The internet let us share data and connect like never before. AI lets us do things we never could — at speeds we'd have thought unimaginable five years ago.

Every "human in the loop" advocate believes in the concept. I'd bet a fraction of them actually insert themselves to any meaningful degree. Give it a few years. Once AI performs review steps as well as humans, the human bottleneck disappears. The process just moves faster without us, and nobody fights to slow it back down.

We're building governance for systems that will outpace the governance before the ink dries. On top of internet infrastructure we've had 25 years to secure and still haven't.

Maybe before we race to govern AI, we should finish the last assignment.

Where should the industry's energy be — catching up on internet-era security gaps, or racing ahead to AI governance?
