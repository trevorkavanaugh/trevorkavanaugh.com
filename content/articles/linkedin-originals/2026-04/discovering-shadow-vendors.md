# Discovering Shadow Vendors - LinkedIn Original

**Posted:** April 14, 2026
**Platform:** LinkedIn
**Author:** Trevor Kavanaugh

---

When I took over vendor management at a previous institution, one question kept nagging at me. What if someone in a business unit just signs a contract with a new vendor and routes the invoices to AP? How would we ever know?

I asked around. Nobody had an answer. Some people vaguely thought there was an integration or a process. There wasn't. AP had no obligation to check with us before paying a new entity for the first time. No form. No approval workflow. Nothing.

If a business unit engaged a vendor and never told us, that vendor would operate indefinitely with no due diligence, no insurance verification, no risk assessment. And we'd have no idea.

🔴 Finding the gap

I reported it as a management self-identified issue. The head of ERM at the time told me it would be a waste of time and effort that wouldn't lead to any discoveries.

My intuition said otherwise. If a gap exists, things fall through it.

I worked with AP to build a new vendor approval form - any new entity that didn't meet specific exemption criteria (SBA referrals, employee reimbursements, etc.) had to be approved by my department before AP would process the invoice. That closed the gap going forward.

But I still needed to know what had already fallen through.

⚠️ Looking backward

Vendors bill monthly, quarterly, or annually. It's rare for a vendor to bill on a cycle longer than 12 months. So 12 months of AP data would surface virtually every active vendor the bank was using.

I asked AP to run every entity through the approval form for 12 months. They said no - too much work, not enough capacity.

So I built a process to compare 12 months of AP payment data against our vendor inventory. Every entity AP had paid that didn't appear in our system got flagged and classified - known vendor, possible vendor, former vendor, or not a vendor.

The results came back. Over 150 entities appeared to be active vendors with no due diligence on file. No risk assessments. No certificates of insurance. Nothing from my department. Because nobody ever told us they existed.

📊 What happened next

I reported to the risk committee that 150+ entities believed to be vendors were operating outside our policies and procedures. The head of ERM who said this would be a waste of time was sitting in that meeting.

It was eventually elevated to senior leadership that this needed to be resolved immediately. I had been trying to get business units to complete reconciliation forms for months with no traction. The pressure that elevation provided gave me an escalation path to the COO and CEO. The entire list was remediated within 90 days.

The gap ERM said wasn't worth investigating had 150 things fall through it.

Has your program ever gone back and validated whether every active vendor is actually in your system - or are you assuming the process catches them all?
