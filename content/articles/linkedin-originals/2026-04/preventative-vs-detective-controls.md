# When an Auditor Got Stuck on Control Type Instead of Control Effectiveness - LinkedIn Original

**Posted:** April 21, 2026
**Platform:** LinkedIn
**Author:** Trevor Kavanaugh

---

Last week we talked about discovering 150+ vendors operating outside our policies. What happened next with the auditor was even more frustrating than the discovery itself.

An external auditor came in to validate that the control gap had been remediated. I walked him through what we built - a new vendor approval form requiring my department's sign-off before AP would process first-time invoices, plus a process to scrub 12 months of AP data against our inventory to catch anything that had already slipped through.

His response: "This control seems detective, not preventative. I'm going to have to fail it."

🔴 The conversation that followed

We ended up in a room - my team, internal audit, and this external auditor - discussing what a "preventative" control would look like for this risk.

Someone suggested: if a vendor isn't in our system, they don't get paid.

I pushed back. That's not preventative either. All that does is introduce a new risk - failing to pay entities we are contractually obligated to pay because someone already signed the contract. The engagement already happened. The horse already left the barn. You're just punishing AP for someone else's noncompliance.

⚠️ The fundamental misunderstanding

There is no such thing as a preventative control for an employee choosing to be noncompliant with policy. Unless you want to assign one dedicated monitor to every employee in the company whose sole job is standing over their shoulder making sure they don't sign a DocuSign or print a contract - you cannot prevent this from happening.

The auditor wanted a control type that doesn't exist for this category of risk. He was so focused on checking the "preventative" box that he couldn't see the actual risk reduction sitting right in front of him.

We went from zero visibility - no process, no monitoring, no way to know if vendors were falling through the cracks - to a system that catches unknown vendors within a billing cycle and flags them for onboarding. That is meaningful risk reduction.

📊 Control type vs control effectiveness

The fixation on whether a control is detective or preventative misses what actually matters - does the control reduce risk?

A detective control that catches 150 unknown vendors and gets them remediated within 90 days reduced more risk than any preventative control that existed before it. Because before it, no control existed at all.

I am a risk manager, not a risk eliminator. Risk exists regardless of what I put in place. My job is to minimize it and make sure it doesn't cause damage to the institution. Demanding a control type that's impossible for a given risk category isn't rigor - it's misunderstanding what risk management actually is.

The auditor eventually moved on. But he never did validate the remediation while I was there.
