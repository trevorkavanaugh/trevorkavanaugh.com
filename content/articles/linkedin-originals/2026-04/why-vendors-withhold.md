# Every Vendor Is Incentivized to Hide Bad News - LinkedIn Original

**Posted:** April 30, 2026
**Platform:** LinkedIn
**Author:** Trevor Kavanaugh

---

Every vendor you work with is incentivized to hide bad news from you. Not because they're malicious. Because the system rewards it.

Think about what happens when a vendor discloses a security incident. Their stock drops. Customers panic. Prospects walk. Competitors use it in sales decks. Regulators show up. Lawsuits follow.

Now think about what happens when they don't disclose. Nothing. At least not immediately. And "not immediately" is all the incentive most organizations need.

🔴 The math works against you

A vendor discovers a breach. From that moment, every day they delay disclosure is a day their stock price holds, their sales pipeline stays intact, and their other clients don't start demanding answers.

Their lawyers advise containment before notification. Their PR team wants to control the narrative. Their board wants full scope before making statements. All rational from their perspective.

The average vendor takes about 25 days to notify affected clients. Banks have regulatory obligations to notify consumers within 36 to 72 hours. Your contract says 24 hours. The vendor's entire incentive structure says wait.

⚠️ It's not just incidents

The incentive to withhold extends far beyond breach notification.

Vendor questionnaires are self-reported. What's the incentive to be honest about control weaknesses when honesty could cost you the contract? A vendor that admits "we don't have a formal patch management cadence" loses deals to the one that writes "patches are applied in accordance with our risk-based methodology" - which could mean the exact same thing.

SOC 2 scoping is vendor-defined. What's the incentive to include every system when a narrower scope means fewer findings and a cleaner report?

Policy documents get redacted before sharing. What's the incentive to give full transparency into your security program when that could expose gaps?

At every stage of due diligence, the vendor's business interests and your risk management interests point in opposite directions.

📊 How does this change?

The system shifts when the incentive to disclose outweighs the incentive to withhold.

Right now, penalties for delayed breach notification are usually smaller than the market punishment for timely disclosure. That math has to flip. When withholding costs more than transparency, behavior changes.

Regulatory pressure helps - mandatory notification windows with real enforcement. Contractual consequences help if they're enforceable at scale. But the most powerful lever might be market expectation. If customers start treating transparency as a competitive advantage and opacity as a red flag, the incentive structure starts to move.

We reward vendors who look clean on paper. We should be rewarding vendors who are honest about where they're not.
