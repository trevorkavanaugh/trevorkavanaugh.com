# Contracts Are Financial Disaster Recovery - LinkedIn Original

**Posted:** April 7, 2026
**Platform:** LinkedIn
**Author:** Trevor Kavanaugh

---

Contracts are financial disaster recovery. They are not risk management.

Every TPRM program spends significant time negotiating SLAs, breach notification windows, liability caps, and indemnification clauses. It feels like risk management. It checks the box for auditors and examiners. But when something actually goes wrong, those clauses don't do what most people think they do.

🔴 The small incident

Your vendor gets breached. Two of your clients' records were exposed. Your contract says the vendor must notify you within 24 hours. They took 25 days.

Are you taking them to court? No. You're sending apology letters to two customers and moving on. The legal fees alone would dwarf any damages. That notification clause you spent three weeks negotiating did nothing to prevent the breach, nothing to accelerate the response, and you were never going to enforce it.

⚠️ The big incident

Now scale it up. Thousands of records. Material harm. Regulatory scrutiny. NOW the contract matters - but only to determine who pays for the wreckage. The breach already happened. Your clients were already impacted. The contract kicks in as a liability allocation tool after the damage is done.

That's what contract clauses actually manage. Financial exposure after failure. They don't prevent failure.

📊 Why vendors drag their feet

The average vendor takes about 25 days to notify clients of an incident. Banks have regulatory obligations to notify consumers within 36 to 72 hours depending on jurisdiction. That math doesn't work, and contract language doesn't fix it.

Vendors delay because they're incentivized to. They want to fully investigate, understand the scope, and control the narrative before disclosing. Every day between discovery and disclosure is a day their stock price holds, their reputation stays intact, and their other clients don't panic. Their lawyers are advising containment while your contract sits in a drawer saying "24 hours."
The incentive structure works against you regardless of what the contract says.

💡 So what are contracts actually good for?

They're good for exactly one thing - establishing financial liability when something catastrophic happens. That matters. When a vendor causes a seven-figure loss, you want indemnification language that holds up.

But most programs treat contract negotiation as risk mitigation. It's downstream cleanup dressed up as upstream prevention. Your examiners see strong contract language and feel comfortable. The risk didn't actually move.

How much of your TPRM program is built around satisfying auditors versus actually reducing risk - and where's the biggest gap between the two?
