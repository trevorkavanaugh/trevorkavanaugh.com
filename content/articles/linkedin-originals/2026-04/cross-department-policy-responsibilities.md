# Cross-Department Policy Responsibilities: The Accountability Gap - LinkedIn Original

**Posted:** April 9, 2026
**Platform:** LinkedIn
**Author:** Trevor Kavanaugh

---

The most dangerous line in any TPRM policy is a responsibility assigned to a department that isn't yours.

At a previous institution, our policy stated that critical-risk vendors - 13 of them - would receive annual site visits. Reasonable expectation. These were SaaS providers handling sensitive data and core banking functions.

I assumed IT was conducting them. My team of two wasn't qualified to site visit software companies and assess technical controls - that's not a TPRM program manager's skillset, that's cybersecurity. And because our risk management functions operated in silos with minimal cross-department communication, I had no visibility into whether they were happening.

They weren't.

🔴 What I found

The last site visit IT conducted was two years prior, during onboarding of a new MSSP. Even our core processor had possibly never been visited. Neither had the other 12 critical vendors on the list. All software companies. All handling our most sensitive data.

Our policy said annually. Reality was never.

⚠️ Why this happens
When I confronted IT, they didn't dispute it. They also didn't change anything. No urgency. No remediation plan. It wasn't in their policy. It wasn't in their objectives. It was a line item in MY department's policy that assigned THEM a responsibility they never agreed to own.

And that's the structural problem nobody talks about. Policy statements that assign obligations across department lines without corresponding accountability on the other side. There's no enforcement mechanism. No violation tracking. No consequence for noncompliance by the department that's supposed to execute.

Your policy says IT conducts site visits. IT's policy says nothing about it. When the examiner asks why site visits weren't completed, they're reading YOUR policy - so they're looking at you.

📊 Where this actually breaks programs

Site visits are one example. But this pattern shows up everywhere. Your policy says business units will notify TPRM before engaging new vendors. Your policy says IT will provide access certifications for critical vendor connections. Your policy says procurement will route contracts through your review process.

Every one of those is a dependency on a department that may not know, may not care, or may not have capacity to comply with what YOUR policy promises.

💡 The fix nobody wants to implement

Second line of defense has a tool for this - violation tracking. When a department fails to execute a responsibility your policy assigns them, document it. Issue it formally. Create a paper trail that separates your program's accountability from their noncompliance.

Most people don't do this because it feels adversarial. It creates friction. But the alternative is owning the finding when their failure shows up in your exam.

How does your program handle policy responsibilities that depend on other departments - do you have enforcement mechanisms, or are you hoping they comply?
