I recently wrote about discovering 150+ vendors operating outside our policies - vendors that had been onboarded, contracted, and paid without ever going through our TPRM program. What happened next, when an external auditor came in to validate our remediation, was even more frustrating than the discovery itself. It exposed something I've seen repeatedly in risk management: the fixation on control classification over control effectiveness.

## The Auditor's Objection

I walked the auditor through what we had built: a new vendor approval form requiring my department's sign-off before accounts payable would process first-time invoices, plus a process to scrub 12 months of AP data against our vendor inventory to catch anything that had already slipped through. It was a real, functioning system that gave us visibility we hadn't had before.

His response: "This control seems detective, not preventative. I'm going to have to fail it."

We ended up in a room - my team, internal audit, and this external auditor - discussing what a "preventative" control would look like for this specific risk.

Someone suggested: if a vendor isn't in our system, they don't get paid.

I pushed back immediately. That's not a preventative control either. All it does is introduce a new risk - failing to pay entities we are contractually obligated to pay because someone upstream already signed the contract without following policy. The engagement already happened. The horse already left the barn. Blocking payment at that point doesn't prevent the policy violation. It punishes accounts payable for someone else's noncompliance, while also exposing the institution to breach of contract liability.

## The Fundamental Misunderstanding

There is no such thing as a genuinely preventative control for an employee choosing to be noncompliant with policy. Unless you want to assign one dedicated monitor to every employee in the company whose sole job is standing over their shoulder making sure they don't sign a DocuSign or print a contract without authorization - you cannot prevent this from happening.

Policy violations rooted in human behavior don't have true preventative controls. They have **deterrent controls** (training, accountability, disciplinary risk), **detective controls** (monitoring, reconciliation, audits), and **corrective controls** (remediation workflows). The audit community broadly understands this distinction. What surprised me was an external auditor who couldn't see it in front of him.

He was so fixated on checking the "preventative" box in his audit program that he couldn't evaluate the actual risk reduction sitting in the room with him. This is the same pattern I've written about in the context of compliance theater - when the form of risk management displaces the substance of it.

## Control Type vs. Control Effectiveness

Let me be specific about what we actually built and what it delivered.

**Before the control:** Zero visibility. No process, no monitoring, no way to know if vendors were falling through the cracks. Unknown vendors could operate indefinitely with no detection mechanism.

**After the control:** Unknown vendors get caught within a billing cycle and flagged for onboarding. We retroactively identified and remediated over 150 vendors who had bypassed the program. We now have a systematic process that runs continuously.

That is meaningful, demonstrable risk reduction. The AP scrub alone was forensic-level work - reconciling a year of payment data against our inventory, identifying every vendor relationship that hadn't been through due diligence, and building a remediation queue. The forward-looking approval workflow closed the gap for future engagements.

A detective control that catches 150 unknown vendors and gets them remediated within 90 days reduced more risk than any preventative control that existed before it - because before it, no control existed at all. You cannot compare a real detective control against a theoretical preventative control and conclude the detective control is insufficient.

## What Risk Management Actually Is

I am a risk manager, not a risk eliminator. Risk exists regardless of what controls I put in place. My job is to minimize it, detect it when it occurs, and make sure it doesn't cause material damage to the institution.

Demanding a control type that is structurally impossible for a given risk category isn't rigor. It's a misunderstanding of what risk management actually does. Audit programs that score controls based on classification type rather than effectiveness produce a perverse incentive - organizations chase the right label instead of building the right control.

The auditor eventually moved on without validating the remediation while I was there. The control continued running. Vendors continued being caught and onboarded. The risk continued declining.

Classification is a useful taxonomy for documentation. It is not a proxy for effectiveness. The question that matters isn't "is this detective or preventative?" It's "does this reduce risk, and by how much?"
